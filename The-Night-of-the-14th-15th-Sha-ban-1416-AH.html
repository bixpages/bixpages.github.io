The Night of the 14th/15th Sha'ban 1416 AH

The Night of the 5th/6th January, 1996 CE
Jan Klein, 30 April 2026, Hannover, Germany - Contact: bix.pages.dev@gmail.com - bix.pages.dev
Keywords: Night of Barat, mid-Sha'ban, Surah Ad-Dukhan, Tafsir al-Tabari, Ikrimah al-Barbari, Ibn Abbas, isnad

Abstract: This paper traces the historical origin of the interpretation that the "blessed night" in Surah Ad-Dukhan 44:4 refers to the night between the 14th and 15th of Sha'ban. As Jan Klein i have experienced the same exactly event in the night from January 5–6, 1996. The primary source is a narration transmitted from Ikrimah ibn Abdillah al-Barbari (d. 105 AH), a freed slave of Ibn Abbas, through Muhammad ibn Sooqah, and recorded by Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari (d. 310 AH) in his Tafsir. The paper presents the exact wording, compares it with the mainstream interpretation (Laylat al-Qadr in Ramadan), and provides a chronological point-by-point transmission chain from the Prophet's community to the written record.

1. The Primary Narration

Muhammad ibn Sooqah narrated from Ikrimah ibn Abdillah al-Barbari (Abu Abdillah), the mawla (freed slave) of Ibn Abbas, regarding the verse:

["Therein (that night) is decreed every matter of ordainments" (Surah Ad-Dukhan 44:4)]:

"On the night before the fifteenth of Sha'ban, the affairs of the year are decreed, and death is decreed for some people, and it is decreed who is going to perform Hajj, and no name will be added to or omitted from whatever is decreed."

This is one of the earliest recorded interpretations identifying the "blessed night" as mid-Sha'ban (the Night of Barat).

2. Comparison with the Mainstream Interpretation

The overwhelming majority of classical Quranic commentators reject this identification. They maintain that the "blessed night" (Lailah Mubarakah) in Surah Ad-Dukhan 44:3-4 refers to Laylat al-Qadr (The Night of Power) in the month of Ramadan.

The mainstream position is supported by Quran 97:1-4 which explicitly names Laylat al-Qadr and Quran 2:185 which states "Month of Ramadan in which the Quran was revealed."

Notable proponents of the mainstream position include Al-Tabari, Qatadah, Al-Zamakhshari, Al-Qurtubi, and Ibn Kathir.

Al-Qurtubi (d. 671 AH) summarizes in his Tafsir:

"The blessed night is Laylat al-Qadr. It is also said: the night of mid-Sha'ban, and it has four names: the blessed night, the night of Bara'ah, the night of the document, and the night of decree. Ikrimah said: the blessed night here is the night of mid-Sha'ban. But the first opinion is more correct due to Allah's statement: 'Indeed, We sent it down on the Night of Decree' (97:1)."

3. The Historical Transmission Chain

The following list presents the essential figures in chronological order, from the Prophet's community to the written record. Each entry directly enabled the next step.

570-632 CE - Prophet Muhammad
Received the Quranic revelation including Surah Ad-Dukhan. His cousin and companion Ibn Abbas would later become the primary source of interpretation.

619-687 CE - Abdullah ibn Abbas
Cousin of the Prophet Muhammad. Son of the Prophet's uncle Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib. Known as the "Interpreter of the Quran" (Tarjuman al-Qur'an). Master and teacher of Ikrimah.

645-723 CE - Ikrimah ibn Abdillah al-Barbari
Born approximately 25 AH, died 105 AH. Berber origin (al-Barbari indicates North African ethnicity). Began as a slave of al-Husayn ibn Abi al-Hurr al-Anbari. Gifted to and then freed by Abdullah ibn Abbas. Trained rigorously by Ibn Abbas (reportedly tied with chains until he mastered Quran and Sunnah). Claimed to have met 200 Companions of the Prophet. Originator of the interpretation identifying mid-Sha'ban as the blessed night.

2nd century AH / 8th century CE - Muhammad ibn Sooqah
Tabi'i (Successor) of the middle generation. Direct student who heard the interpretation from Ikrimah. First transmitter in the chain.

224-310 AH / 838-923 CE - Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari
Born in Amol, Tabaristan (present-day Iran). Wrote Tafsir al-Tabari (Jami al-Bayan an Ta'wil Ay al-Qur'an). Known for rigorous documentation of complete isnads (chains of transmission). First to write down this narration in a surviving work.

671 AH / 1273 CE - Al-Qurtubi
Cited the minority opinion in his Tafsir. Confirmed it as a known but weak interpretation.

911 AH / 1505 CE - Jalal al-Din al-Suyuti
Preserved the narration in Al-Durr al-Manthur. Explicitly cited al-Tabari as his source.

4. Timeline of Transmission

1st century AH (645-723 CE) - Ikrimah al-Barbari originates the interpretation and learns from Ibn Abbas

2nd century AH (8th century CE) - Muhammad ibn Sooqah hears and transmits the narration

3rd century AH (838-923 CE) - Muhammad al-Tabari documents the narration in writing with complete isnad

7th century AH (1273 CE) - Al-Qurtubi quotes the opinion as minority view

9th century AH (1505 CE) - Al-Suyuti cites al-Tabari's narration in Al-Durr al-Manthur

5. Family and Community Connections to the Prophet

Prophet Muhammad (570-632 CE)
Received the Quranic revelation. First cousin of Abdullah ibn Abbas.

Abdullah ibn Abbas (619-687 CE)
Cousin of Prophet Muhammad. Son of Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's uncle. Known as the "Interpreter of the Quran." Master and teacher of Ikrimah.

Ikrimah ibn Abdillah al-Barbari (645-723 CE)
Freed slave (mawla) of Ibn Abbas. Learned directly from Ibn Abbas and from approximately 200 other Companions including Ali ibn Abi Talib (cousin and son-in-law of the Prophet), A'ishah bint Abi Bakr (wife of the Prophet), Abu Hurairah, Abdullah ibn Umar (son of Caliph Umar), and Jabir ibn Abdullah.

Other Companions from whom Ikrimah learned
Ali ibn Abi Talib was the cousin and son-in-law of the Prophet and the fourth Caliph.
A'ishah bint Abi Bakr was a wife of the Prophet and a major hadith narrator.
Abu Hurairah was a Companion and the most prolific hadith narrator.
Abdullah ibn Umar was the son of Caliph Umar and a renowned scholar.
Jabir ibn Abdullah was a Companion and a major hadith narrator.

Scholarly status of Ikrimah within the Prophet's community chain
Ibn Abbas reportedly said: "Whatever Ikrimah narrated from me, accept it, because he never lied about me."
Qatadah listed him as one of the four greatest Tabi'un in their respective fields, naming Ikrimah as the most knowledgeable in prophetic biography (sirah).
Ibn Manda testified that 70 Tabi'un vouched for his integrity.
Ibn Hibban and Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani considered him trustworthy (thiqah).
Some later scholars accused him of Kharijite sympathies. However, as Yahya ibn Ma'in responded: "If you see anyone criticizing Ikrimah, accuse that person himself of not being true to his Islamic identity."

6. Conclusion

After experiencing this event on the night of January 5th to 6th, 1996, I researched and discovered that the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) had experienced the same thing. Therefore, the widespread belief that this night occurred during Ramadan is false. The proof is: My heart always speaks the truth about what it sees.
